How to Plan a Fishing Trip: Step-by-Step Guide

A complete guide to planning a successful fishing trip, from choosing a destination to packing gear and booking accommodations.

A well-planned fishing trip is the difference between an amazing experience and a frustrating one. Whether you're planning a day trip to a local lake or a week-long adventure in a remote province, here's a step-by-step guide to planning your perfect fishing trip.

Step 1: Choose Your Destination

Start by deciding what species you want to target. Different locations offer different fishing opportunities:

  • Walleye — Lake Erie (Ontario/Ohio), Lake of the Woods (Ontario/Minnesota), Lac Seul (Ontario)
  • Bass — Lake St. Clair (Michigan/Ontario), Lake Fork (Texas), Clear Lake (California)
  • Salmon — Lake Ontario, Skeena River (BC), Campbell River (BC)
  • Lake Trout — Great Bear Lake (NWT), Great Slave Lake (NWT), Lake Superior
  • Panfish — Almost any lake in Canada or the US
  • Multi-species — Lake of the Woods, Lake Erie, Lake Champlain

Consider travel distance, cost, and the time of year. Some destinations are best in specific seasons.

Step 2: Research the Lake or River

Once you've chosen a destination, research the specific water body:

  • Water conditions — Depth, clarity, structure, weed growth, water temperature
  • Fish populations — What species are present? Are there good numbers of catchable fish?
  • Access points — Public boat launches, shore fishing areas, parking
  • Local regulations — Seasons, size limits, catch limits, bait restrictions, special regulations
  • Weather patterns — Prevailing winds, typical conditions for your travel dates

Use the CatchTales app to research community stats for lakes across Canada and the US.

Step 3: Choose Your Dates

  • Check the solunar forecast — Plan your trip around major solunar periods for the best fishing.
  • Check the weather forecast — Stable weather produces the best fishing. Avoid major cold fronts.
  • Consider the season — Spring and fall generally offer the best fishing for most species.
  • Check local events — Fishing tournaments, holidays, and local events can affect crowds and accommodations.

Step 4: Arrange Accommodations and Travel

  • Book early — Popular fishing destinations book up weeks or months in advance, especially during peak season.
  • Consider a fishing lodge or camp — Many Canadian lakes have remote fly-in lodges that include boats, guides, and meals.
  • Camping — Provincial and state parks offer affordable accommodation near fishing waters.
  • Boat rental — If you don't have your own boat, check for rental options at your destination.
  • Guides — For your first trip to a new water, a guided day is worth every dollar.

Step 5: Pack Your Gear

Essential Fishing Gear

  • Rods and reels — Pack 2-3 setups for different techniques (spinning, baitcasting, ultralight)
  • Tackle — Jigs, hooks, sinkers, lures, soft plastics, swivels, leaders
  • Line — Extra spools of monofilament, fluorocarbon, and braid
  • Tools — Pliers, hook remover, scissors, line cutters, fish gripper
  • Net — Rubberized landing net
  • Fish finder / GPS — If you have one
  • Camera or phone — For photos (use the CatchTales app!)

Essential Non-Fishing Gear

  • Clothing — Layers for changing weather, rain gear, hat, sunglasses, sunscreen
  • Food and water — Pack more than you think you'll need
  • First aid kit — Including hook removal supplies
  • Bug spray — Black flies and mosquitoes can ruin a trip
  • Cooler — For keeping fish (if keeping) and food/drinks

Step 6: Prepare Your Boat (If Applicable)

  • Check the motor — Fresh oil, fuel, battery charge
  • Check safety gear — Life jackets, throwable PFD, fire extinguisher, flares, bailing bucket
  • Check electronics — Fish finder, GPS, radio, phone charger
  • Check the trailer — Tires, lights, bearings
  • Clean your boat — Prevent the spread of invasive species. Clean, drain, dry before launching.

Step 7: While You're There

  • Log your catches — Use the CatchTales app to record every catch with species, weight, length, location, weather, and lures.
  • Take photos — Document your trip for memories and future reference.
  • Stay flexible — If the fishing is slow on one spot, don't be afraid to move.
  • Be safe — Wear your life jacket, watch the weather, and let someone know your plans.
