Fishing Knots Every Angler Should Know

12 essential knots with step-by-step diagrams, video tutorials, line-type compatibility, and common mistakes explained for beginners.

A fishing knot is the weakest link in your setup. A poorly tied knot can lose you a trophy fish, while a well-tied one gives you confidence that every cast might be the one. The good news? You only need a handful of knots to cover almost every fishing situation.

This guide covers 12 knots organized from basic hook knots to specialty rigs. Each knot includes step-by-step SVG diagrams, a video demonstration, line compatibility info, and common mistakes to avoid.

Before you start tying:

  • Wet the line before tightening — reduces friction heat that weakens the knot
  • Pull slowly and firmly — fast, jerky pulls can weaken the knot
  • Trim tag ends to ¼ inch (about 6mm) — too long catches weeds, too short can slip
  • Test every knot by pulling hard before fishing
  • Retie after a big fish, snag, or every few hours — knots weaken with use

1. Palomar Knot

5/5 Strongest
Hooks, swivels, lures Mono, fluoro, braid ⏱️ 30 seconds

The Palomar is widely considered the strongest and most reliable knot for tying terminal tackle. It's simple, fast, and retains nearly 100% of your line's breaking strength. If you learn only one knot, make it this one.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
MonofilamentExcellentWorks with all pound tests
FluorocarbonExcellentPerfect — doesn't slip like some knots
BraidedExcellentHolds well, but can be bulky with heavy braid (50lb+)

Common Mistakes

  • Not wetting the line — heat from friction weakens the knot. Always moisten before pulling tight.
  • Only passing the loop through the eye once — the Palomar requires the line to pass through the hook eye twice.
  • Pulling too fast — pull slowly and steadily to let the knot seat evenly.
  • Not trimming the tag end — leaving too much tag can catch weeds and debris.

2. Improved Clinch Knot

4/5 Very Strong
Hooks, swivels, lures Mono, fluoro (not braid) ⏱️ 20 seconds

The Improved Clinch is the classic all-purpose fishing knot. Generations of anglers learned this one first. It's reliable, fast to tie, and works beautifully with monofilament and fluorocarbon lines. Less reliable with braid — use the Palomar for that.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
MonofilamentExcellentClassic use — works perfectly
FluorocarbonGoodUse 7+ wraps; fluoro is slicker and needs extra friction
BraidedNot RecommendedSlippery braid can pull through. Use Palomar instead

Common Mistakes

  • Too few wraps — 5 wraps minimum for mono, 7+ for fluorocarbon.
  • Skipping the "improved" step — passing the tag through the second loop is what makes it "improved" and doubles the strength.
  • Not cinching tight enough — the knot should be snug against the hook eye. Push it tight before your final pull.

3. Uni Knot (Duncan Loop)

5/5 Strongest
Hooks, swivels, lures All line types ⏱️ 25 seconds

The Uni Knot is incredibly versatile — it works well with mono, fluoro, AND braid. You can also use it to join two lines together (see Double Uni below). Many anglers consider it the only knot they need.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
MonofilamentExcellent5-6 wraps is perfect
FluorocarbonGood6-8 wraps recommended due to slickness
BraidedExcellentHolds braid very well — a top choice for braid to swivel

Common Mistakes

  • Pulling the wrong end — pull the tag end to tighten the coils first, THEN pull the mainline to seat against the eye.
  • Not keeping even tension — the coils need to stay parallel as you tighten. Let them overlap and the knot weakens.
  • Skipping the lube — more than any other knot, the Uni needs moisture to seat its coils properly.

4. Snell Knot

5/5 Strongest
Bait hooks, circle hooks Mono, fluoro ⏱️ 35 seconds

The Snell is unique — instead of pulling against the hook eye, the line runs along the hook shank. This creates a direct pull that improves hook penetration. Essential for bait fishing with circle hooks or J-hooks.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
MonofilamentExcellentTraditional choice for snelling
FluorocarbonGoodWorks with extra wraps (7-8)
BraidedTrickySlick braid can slip on the shank. Use a shrink tube or avoid

Common Mistakes

  • Wrapping in the wrong direction — wraps must go toward the hook point, not toward the eye.
  • Not keeping wraps tight — loose wraps will slide and the knot fails. Hold tension as you go.
  • Using on the wrong hooks — Snell only works with eyed hooks. Not for hooks with flattened or sliced shanks.

5. Double Uni Knot

4/5 Very Strong
Joining two lines All types, different diameters ⏱️ 45 seconds

Tie a Uni knot on each line, then slide them together. The Double Uni is the most beginner-friendly way to join two lines. It works with different line types and diameters — great for tying a leader onto your mainline.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
Mono → MonoExcellentSame diameter works best
Mono → FluoroGoodAdd extra wraps on the fluoro side (it's slicker)
Braid → Mono/FluoroGoodUse 6+ wraps on braid, 5 on mono/fluoro
Heavy difference (>2x)WeakIf one line is much thicker than the other, try the FG Knot instead

Common Mistakes

  • Tightening one side fully before the other — both knots should be snug but not tight before you slide them together.
  • Not enough overlap — 6-8 inches minimum, or the knots won't have room to form.
  • Uneven wraps — if one line is much thicker, add more wraps to the thinner line for balance.

6. Blood Knot

4/5 Very Strong
Leader to tippet, same diameter Mono, fluoro ⏱️ 50 seconds

The Blood Knot is the standard for joining two pieces of similar-diameter line. It's the go-to knot for building tapered leaders in fly fishing, but useful for any situation where you need a smooth, strong connection between two lines of similar size.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
Mono → MonoExcellentBest when diameters are within 0.05mm
Fluoro → FluoroGoodWorks, but the knot can be bulky — wet thoroughly
Mono → FluoroFairDifferent materials can slip. Good tight, but test hard
BraidNot RecommendedBraid doesn't grip itself well in this knot. Use Double Uni

Common Mistakes

  • Mismatched diameters — the Blood Knot works best when both lines are similar thickness. More than a 0.1mm difference and it won't seat properly.
  • Uneven wraps — same number of wraps on each side is critical for balance.
  • Pulling too fast — ease the knot together slowly so the wraps don't overlap.

7. Surgeon's Knot

3/5 Good
Quick leader connection Any lines, different diameters ⏱️ 15 seconds

The Surgeon's Knot is the fastest way to join two lines. It's not the strongest, but it's more than adequate for most fishing and works well with very different line diameters. Perfect for quickly adding a leader on the water.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
Any → AnyGoodWorks with any combination, including braid to mono
Very different diametersGoodPerforms better than Blood Knot with mismatched lines
BraidedGoodPass braid through loop an extra time for security

Common Mistakes

  • Only passing through once — the double pass (two times through the loop) is what makes it a Surgeon's Knot, not a simple overhand.
  • Not wetting the line — with two lines passing through the same knot, friction is high and heat damage is a real risk.
  • Uneven tension when pulling — pull all four ends evenly so the knot seats symmetrically.

8. FG Knot

5/5 Strongest
Braid to fluorocarbon leader Braid + fluoro/mono ⏱️ 90 seconds (slower)

The FG Knot is the gold standard for joining braided mainline to a fluorocarbon leader. It's incredibly thin (passes through rod guides smoothly), retains nearly 100% of line strength, and is trusted by serious anglers worldwide. It takes practice, but it's worth mastering.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
Braid → FluoroExcellentThis is what the FG was designed for — unbeatable
Braid → MonoExcellentWorks just as well with mono leaders
Mono → MonoOverkillWorks, but easier knots (Double Uni) are just as strong

Common Mistakes

  • Not keeping tension — the FG relies on constant tension on the braid wraps. Release tension even once and the whole knot unravels.
  • Too few wraps — 15-20 half-wraps minimum for a secure FG.
  • Giving up too soon — it's frustrating at first. Practice with thick braid and fluoro before moving to thinner lines.
  • Not cinching the half hitches — finish with 3-4 half hitches on the braid side, or the knot can work loose.

9. Rapala Knot

4/5 Very Strong
Topwater lures, minnow baits Mono, fluoro ⏱️ 35 seconds

Creates a fixed loop that lets your lure swing freely. Named after the Rapala lure company, this knot gives minnow-style baits their signature action. The loop lets the lure move side-to-side instead of being constrained by a tight knot.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
MonofilamentExcellentClassic use — loop holds well
FluorocarbonGoodCan be stiff — warm the fluoro slightly with your fingers before tying
BraidedFairBraid can slip in the overhand. Use Non-Slip Mono Loop instead

Common Mistakes

  • Loop too large or too small — aim for a loop about ¼ inch (6mm) diameter. Too big and it catches weeds; too small and it restricts lure action.
  • Passing tag through overhand in wrong direction — the tag must go from the hook side back toward the reel.
  • Tightening before the wraps are seated — snug the wraps first, then pull the mainline to close the overhand knot.

10. Non-Slip Mono Loop

4/5 Very Strong
Any lure where free movement matters Mono, fluoro, braid ⏱️ 35 seconds

The Non-Slip Mono Loop (also called the Kreh Loop) creates a fixed loop that won't slip or tighten under pressure. It's more reliable than the Rapala Knot with braided line and works beautifully with all line types. Perfect for jerkbaits, poppers, and any lure where natural action is critical.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
MonofilamentExcellentAs the name says — designed for mono
FluorocarbonGoodWorks well; wet thoroughly before tightening
BraidedGoodHolds better than Rapala Knot with braid. Add 1-2 extra wraps

Common Mistakes

  • Wrapping in the wrong direction — wraps must go toward the hook eye, not away from it.
  • Not passing the tag back through the big loop — this is the step that locks the knot. Without it, the loop will tighten under pressure.
  • Loop too large — a ¼ to ½ inch loop (6-12mm) is ideal. Too large and it can catch on weeds or affect casting.

11. Dropper Loop

4/5 Very Strong
Multi-hook rigs, paternoster rigs Mono, fluoro ⏱️ 40 seconds

The Dropper Loop creates a branch off your mainline that stands out at a right angle — perfect for multi-hook bait rigs, paternoster setups, or adding a second fly. The loop holds its shape under tension and won't collapse.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
MonofilamentExcellentBest with medium to heavy mono (8-30lb)
FluorocarbonGoodWorks but the loop may be stiffer
BraidedNot RecommendedThe twists won't hold shape in slick braid. Use a separate leader

Common Mistakes

  • Not enough twists — 5-6 twists minimum. Too few and the loop will pull out under tension.
  • Pulling the wrong end — pull both mainline ends away from each other to tighten, not the loop itself.
  • Loop facing the wrong way — the dropper loop should stick out perpendicular to the mainline. If it lies flat, the twists weren't even.

12. Arbor Knot

3/5 Good
Spooling line onto reel All line types ⏱️ 10 seconds

The Arbor Knot is the first knot every new angler ties — it attaches your fishing line to the reel spool. It's incredibly simple but must be tied correctly or the spool will spin without winding line.

Video Tutorial

Line Compatibility

Line TypeRatingNotes
All typesExcellentWorks equally well with mono, fluoro, and braid

Common Mistakes

  • Not wrapping around the arbor first — if you tie the knot without wrapping the arbor, the spool can spin freely inside the line.
  • Not pulling tight enough — a loose Arbor Knot will slip when you start reeling under tension.
  • Adding extra wraps — one wrap around the arbor is sufficient. Extra wraps can create lumps that affect casting.

How to Practice

Learning knots takes repetition. Here's the fastest way to get good:

  1. Start with thick rope — use ⅛-inch (3mm) paracord or an old shoelace to learn the movements. You can see what the knot is doing.
  2. Move to fishing line — once you can tie it in rope, switch to 12-15lb monofilament. The thinner line changes how the knot feels.
  3. Practice blindfolded — seriously. If you can tie it without looking, you can tie it in low light, rough water, or fading daylight.
  4. Watch one video, pause, tie, repeat — watch 5 seconds of the video, pause, tie that step. Don't try to memorize the whole knot at once.
  5. Test every knot — before you trust a knot on the water, test it by pulling hard at home. If it fails, you learn without losing a fish.
