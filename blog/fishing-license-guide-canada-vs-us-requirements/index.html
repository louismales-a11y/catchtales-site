Fishing License Guide: Canada vs US Requirements

A complete guide to fishing license requirements across Canada and the United States. What you need, where to get it, and how much it costs.

Before you cast a line, you need a fishing license. Requirements vary by province and state, and the rules can be confusing — especially if you're crossing borders. Here's a guide to fishing licenses across Canada and the United States.

Fishing Licenses in Canada

Who Needs a License

In all Canadian provinces and territories, anglers aged 18-64 need a valid fishing license. Residents 65+ and youth under 18 often need a license but at reduced or no cost (varies by province). Non-residents always need a license.

Provincial Breakdown

Ontario

Resident: $27 (1-year sport), $13 (1-year conservation). Available online at ontario.ca/fishing.

Non-resident: $57 (1-year sport), $31 (1-year conservation), $28 (8-day), $14 (1-day).

Ontario has a conservation license with reduced catch limits and a sport license with full limits.

British Columbia

Resident: $36 (1-year). Available online at fishing.gov.bc.ca.

Non-resident: $55 (1-year), $23 (8-day), $11 (1-day).

BC also requires a separate tidal waters license for salmon fishing in coastal waters: $8 (5-day), $18 (1-year) for non-residents.

Quebec

Resident: $37 (1-year). Available online or at licence vendors.

Non-resident: $63 (1-year), $35 (7-day), $20 (3-day).

Quebec also requires a separate salmon license for Atlantic salmon fishing.

Other Provinces

Most other provinces offer similar pricing: $25-40 for residents, $40-70 for non-residents. Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, and the Maritime provinces all have online purchasing options.

Fishing Licenses in the United States

Who Needs a License

In all US states, anglers aged 16+ (varies by state) need a fishing license. Most states offer reduced-cost or free licenses for seniors, youth, and military.

State Breakdown (Popular Fishing States)

Florida

Resident: $17 (1-year freshwater), $17 (1-year saltwater), $32 (combo).

Non-resident: $47 (1-year freshwater), $30 (3-day freshwater), $17 (1-day freshwater).

Available at GoOutdoorsFlorida.com.

Texas

Resident: $30 (1-year freshwater), $35 (1-year saltwater), $42 (combo).

Non-resident: $58 (1-year freshwater), $12 (1-day freshwater).

Available at tpwd.texas.gov.

Michigan

Resident: $26 (1-year), $11 (1-day).

Non-resident: $68 (1-year), $10 (1-day).

Available at michigan.gov/dnr.

Minnesota

Resident: $25 (1-year), $22 (1-year for seniors).

Non-resident: $51 (1-year), $22 (3-day), $12 (1-day).

Available at dnr.state.mn.us.

Important Things to Know

  • Licenses are not transferable — Each angler must have their own license.
  • Carry your license with you — Conservation officers check licenses regularly. Digital copies on your phone are accepted in most areas.
  • Special stamps and tags — Some species require additional stamps: salmon (BC, Quebec, Great Lakes), trout stamps (some US states), and walleye tags (some Canadian waters).
  • Cross-border fishing — If fishing in both countries on the same trip, you need a license for each country.
  • Check regulations — Each province/state has specific seasons, size limits, catch limits, and bait restrictions. Always check before you fish.
