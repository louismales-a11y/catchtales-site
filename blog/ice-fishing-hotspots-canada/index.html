Ice Fishing Hotspots in Canada: Top 10 Lakes

Discover the best ice fishing lakes in Canada for walleye, lake trout, perch, and pike. Locations, species, and tips for a successful ice fishing trip.

Ice fishing is a Canadian tradition. From December through March, anglers across the country head onto frozen lakes in search of walleye, lake trout, perch, and pike. Here are the top ice fishing destinations in Canada.

1. Lake Simcoe, Ontario

Target species: Lake Trout, Whitefish, Perch

Lake Simcoe is one of the most popular ice fishing destinations in Canada. The lake's south end, particularly around Cook's Bay and Kempenfelt Bay, offers excellent lake trout and whitefish fishing. Perch fishing can be exceptional, with good numbers of jumbo perch available. Ice huts, rentals, and guide services are widely available. January through February is prime time.

2. Lake of the Woods, Ontario

Target species: Walleye, Sauger, Lake Trout, Northern Pike

Lake of the Woods offers phenomenal ice fishing for walleye. The lake's diverse structure — rocky reefs, sandy flats, and deep basins — holds good numbers of fish. The area around Sioux Narrows and Kenora is particularly productive. Many outfitters offer ice hut rentals and guide services.

3. Bay of Quinte, Ontario

Target species: Walleye

The Bay of Quinte is famous for its trophy walleye fishery, and ice fishing is no exception. The upper and middle bays produce excellent action for walleye in the 3-8 pound range. Jigging with spoons and minnows is the most productive technique. Late January through February is prime time.

4. Lake Winnipeg, Manitoba

Target species: Walleye (Pickeral), Lake Trout, Northern Pike

Lake Winnipeg offers incredible ice fishing for greenback walleye — a strain known for their size and fighting ability. The lake's vast size means there's plenty of water to explore. The south basin near Winnipeg Beach and the north basin near Hecla Island are popular areas.

5. Great Slave Lake, Northwest Territories

Target species: Lake Trout, Northern Pike, Arctic Grayling

Great Slave Lake offers some of the most remote and pristine ice fishing in Canada. The lake holds massive lake trout — fish over 40 pounds are caught regularly. This is a destination for serious ice anglers willing to brave extreme cold for trophy fish. February through March is the best window.

6. Lac St. Jean, Quebec

Target species: Walleye, Lake Trout, Northern Pike

Lac St. Jean in Quebec is known for its excellent ice fishing, particularly for walleye. The lake's diverse structure and abundant forage produce healthy fish populations. The area around Roberval and Alma offers good access and services.

7. Lake Nipissing, Ontario

Target species: Walleye, Perch, Pike

Lake Nipissing near North Bay offers excellent ice fishing with easy access. The lake is shallow and productive, with good numbers of walleye and perch. The West Arm and the area near the French River outlet are popular spots. Many ice hut rentals are available right from the shore.

8. Lesser Slave Lake, Alberta

Target species: Walleye, Northern Pike, Lake Trout, Perch

Lesser Slave Lake in northern Alberta offers excellent ice fishing for multiple species. The lake is known for its walleye and northern pike, with good numbers of quality fish. The south shore near the town of Slave Lake provides good access.

9. Lake Scugog, Ontario

Target species: Perch, Crappie, Walleye

Lake Scugog, north of Oshawa, is a popular ice fishing destination for perch and crappie. The lake's shallow, weedy waters produce good numbers of panfish. It's a great option for a day trip from the Toronto area.

10. Tobin Lake, Saskatchewan

Target species: Walleye, Northern Pike

Tobin Lake in Saskatchewan is renowned for its trophy walleye. The lake consistently produces fish over 10 pounds, making it a bucket-list destination for serious walleye anglers. Ice fishing here peaks in February and March.

Ice Fishing Safety Tips

  • Check ice thickness — 4 inches of clear ice is minimum for walking, 12 inches for vehicles. Always check local conditions.
  • Dress in layers — Merino wool base, insulating mid-layer, waterproof outer shell. Don't forget hand and foot warmers.
  • Bring safety gear — Ice picks, throw rope, PFD, and a spud bar to test ice ahead of you.
  • Fish with a buddy — Never ice fish alone. Let someone know your location and expected return time.
