Fishing in Saskatchewan: Top Species and Best Spots

Saskatchewan is walleye country — the province has more lakes and rivers than any other in Canada, with world-class fishing for walleye, pike, and lake trout.

Saskatchewan is home to over 100,000 lakes and more fishable water than any other Canadian province. The Saskatchewan Record Fish Registry tracks over 40 species, and the province's northern waters produce trophy-sized fish year after year. From the fertile prairie reservoirs to the deep, clear shield lakes of the north, here are the top species and where to find them.

1. Tobin Lake (Walleye & Northern Pike)

Best time: May through September, and winter ice fishing

Tobin Lake, formed by the Squaw Rapids Dam on the Saskatchewan River, is widely considered one of the best walleye lakes in Canada. The lake regularly produces trophy walleye over 10 pounds, with fish in the 3-6 pound range common. The flooded timber and structure provide excellent habitat. Tobin Lake is also famous for its northern pike fishery, with fish exceeding 20 pounds caught every year. Spring and fall are the prime seasons. Jigging with minnows and trolling crankbaits are the most productive techniques.

2. Lake Diefenbaker (Walleye, Northern Pike & Rainbow Trout)

Best time: May through September

Lake Diefenbaker is a massive prairie reservoir that offers excellent fishing for walleye, northern pike, and rainbow trout. The lake's stained water and abundant structure make it highly productive. Walleye in the 2-5 pound range are common, with larger fish available for those who put in the time. The rainbow trout fishery is unique among Saskatchewan lakes — these fish are hard-fighting and can reach 10+ pounds. The lake also holds burbot and yellow perch. The Qu'Appelle River arm and the Dam area are particularly productive.

3. Lac La Ronge (Walleye, Northern Pike & Lake Trout)

Best time: June through August

Lac La Ronge, in the boreal shield of northern Saskatchewan, is a massive, clear lake that offers exceptional fishing for walleye, northern pike, and lake trout. The lake's rocky structure, deep basins, and countless islands provide perfect habitat. Walleye in the 2-5 pound range are abundant. Lake trout fishing is excellent in the deeper basins, with fish in the 5-15 pound range common. The lake is accessible via Highway 2 and has numerous fly-in lodges for a remote experience.

4. Lake Athabasca (Lake Trout & Arctic Grayling)

Best time: June through August

Lake Athabasca, straddling the Saskatchewan-Alberta border, is one of the largest lakes in North America and offers world-class lake trout fishing. The lake's deep, clear, cold water produces trophy lake trout that regularly exceed 30 pounds. The Saskatchewan side, accessible via the community of Uranium City or by float plane, offers a true wilderness experience. Arctic grayling are abundant in the lake's tributaries, providing excellent fly fishing opportunities. This is remote, big-water fishing at its finest.

5. Cree Lake (Lake Trout & Northern Pike)

Best time: July through August

Cree Lake, located in the heart of the Saskatchewan shield, is one of the premier trophy lake trout destinations in North America. The lake is deep, clear, and incredibly productive, with lake trout regularly exceeding 40 pounds. The lake is fly-in only, accessed through lodges that have operated here for decades. Northern pike in Cree Lake also reach trophy sizes, with fish over 30 pounds possible. This is a true bucket-list destination for serious anglers.

6. Last Mountain Lake (Walleye & Northern Pike)

Best time: May through September, and winter ice fishing

Last Mountain Lake, in southern Saskatchewan, is one of the province's most popular fishing destinations. The lake's shallow, productive waters support healthy populations of walleye and northern pike. Walleye in the 2-4 pound range are common, with larger fish available in the deeper basins. The lake is easily accessible from Regina and Saskatoon, making it a favourite weekend destination. Ice fishing is excellent, particularly for walleye and burbot.

7. Churchill River System (Walleye, Northern Pike & Arctic Grayling)

Best time: June through August

The Churchill River system flows across northern Saskatchewan and offers exceptional fishing in a remote, pristine setting. The river's current, rapids, and deep pools hold good numbers of walleye and northern pike. Arctic grayling are abundant in the clear tributaries. The river is accessible by canoe or boat from several communities, including La Ronge and Ile-a-la-Crosse. This is a popular route for multi-day canoe trips that combine fishing with wilderness adventure.

8. Meadow Lake (Walleye & Northern Pike)

Best time: May through August

Meadow Lake Provincial Park in northwestern Saskatchewan offers excellent fishing in a beautiful setting. Meadow Lake itself holds good populations of walleye and northern pike, with fish in the 2-4 pound range common. The park's numerous smaller lakes offer a variety of fishing opportunities, including yellow perch and whitefish. The park is well-equipped with campgrounds and boat launches, making it a great family fishing destination.

9. Black Lake (Lake Trout & Arctic Grayling)

Best time: July through August

Black Lake, in the far north of Saskatchewan near the Northwest Territories border, offers pristine fishing for lake trout and Arctic grayling. The lake is large, deep, and clear, with excellent lake trout populations. Fish in the 5-15 pound range are common. The lake's tributaries offer excellent Arctic grayling fishing on light tackle and flies. Access is by float plane only, providing a true wilderness experience. Several lodges operate on the lake.

10. South Saskatchewan River (Walleye & Lake Sturgeon)

Best time: June through September

The South Saskatchewan River flows through Saskatoon and offers excellent fishing for walleye and lake sturgeon. The walleye population has rebounded strongly in recent years, with fish in the 2-5 pound range now common throughout the river. Lake sturgeon, while protected and catch-and-release only, are present in good numbers and can reach impressive sizes. The river's current, deep pools, and sandbars provide diverse habitat. Jigging, bottom bouncing, and drift fishing are all productive techniques.

Fishing Tips for Saskatchewan

  • Get a licence — All anglers 16+ need a Saskatchewan fishing licence. Buy online at nirb.saskatchewan.ca. Non-residents need a separate licence.
  • Book ahead — Northern fly-in lodges book months in advance, especially for lake trout season. Plan your trip early.
  • Check the limits — Saskatchewan has strict possession limits, especially for lake trout and walleye. Some waters have reduced limits or catch-and-release only. Always check current regulations.
  • Bring bug spray — Northern Saskatchewan has legendary mosquito and black fly populations in June and July. A head net and high-DEET repellent are essential.
  • Watch the weather — Saskatchewan lakes can get dangerously rough in high winds. Northern lakes are particularly exposed. Always check the forecast and head for shelter if conditions deteriorate.
