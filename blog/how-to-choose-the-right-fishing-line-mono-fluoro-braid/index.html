How to Choose the Right Fishing Line: Mono, Fluoro, Braid

A complete guide to choosing fishing line — monofilament, fluorocarbon, and braided line — for different fishing situations in Canada and the US.

Choosing the right fishing line is one of the most important decisions you'll make as an angler. The wrong line can cost you fish; the right line makes everything easier. Here's a complete guide to the three main line types.

Monofilament (Mono)

Best For: Beginners, Topwater, Cranking, General Purpose

Monofilament is the original modern fishing line. It's a single strand of nylon that stretches under pressure.

Advantages:

  • Cheapest option — great for beginners
  • Stretches — good shock absorption for fighting fish, good for crankbaits (allows fish to load the rod)
  • Floats — ideal for topwater lures
  • Easy to tie knots — the most knot-friendly line type
  • More abrasion resistant than braid, less than fluoro for the same diameter
  • Good for most general-purpose fishing

Disadvantages:

  • Stretches — less sensitivity for feeling bites in deep water
  • Degrades in sunlight — needs to be replaced more often
  • Higher diameter for the same strength — less line capacity on spools
  • Memory — can coil on the spool if not changed regularly

Recommended Uses:

Beginners, topwater lures, crankbaits, general fishing, live bait rigs.

Fluorocarbon (Fluoro)

Best For: Clear Water, Finesse Fishing, Leaders

Fluorocarbon is a denser, stiffer material that is nearly invisible underwater.

Advantages:

  • Nearly invisible underwater — the biggest advantage for clear-water fishing
  • Sinks — ideal for jigs, drop-shot, and other bottom-contact presentations
  • Low stretch — excellent sensitivity for feeling bites in deep water
  • More abrasion resistant than mono — good for fishing around rocks and wood
  • UV resistant — lasts longer than mono

Disadvantages:

  • Expensive — costs 2-3x more than monofilament
  • Stiff — can have memory issues, harder to tie knots (wet your knots!)
  • Sinks — not suitable for topwater lures
  • Less shock absorption — can break on hard-striking fish if drag is too tight

Recommended Uses:

Clear water fishing, drop-shot rigs, jigs, deep-water presentations, leader material for braid.

Braid (Braided Line)

Best For: Heavy Cover, Deep Water, Long Casting, Big Fish

Braided line is made of multiple strands of synthetic fibers (usually Dyneema or Spectra) woven together.

Advantages:

  • Extremely strong for its diameter — you can use 30 lb braid with the diameter of 8 lb mono
  • Virtually no stretch — maximum sensitivity and instant hooksets
  • Great line capacity — fits much more line on your spool
  • Excellent for long casting — casts farther than mono or fluoro of the same strength
  • Lasts a long time — if well cared for, braid can last multiple seasons
  • Works well with baitcasting reels

Disadvantages:

  • Highly visible in water — not suitable as a main line in clear water without a leader
  • Expensive — generally the most expensive line type
  • No stretch — can pull hooks on hard-mouthed fish if drag isn't set properly
  • Hard to tie knots — requires special knots (Palomar, uni-to-uni for connections)
  • Can dig into itself on the spool under heavy pressure
  • Can cut into guides and line rollers if frayed

Recommended Uses:

Flipping and pitching in heavy cover, fishing deep water (50+ ft), frogging in thick vegetation, trolling, main line for baitcasting. Always use a fluorocarbon or mono leader in clear water.

Line Selection by Situation

  • Clear water, finesse fishing → Fluorocarbon (8-12 lb)
  • Topwater lures → Monofilament (12-17 lb)
  • Heavy cover, flipping → Braid (30-65 lb)
  • Deep crankbaits → Monofilament (10-15 lb) for stretch, or fluorocarbon for sensitivity
  • Jigs and worms (clear water) → Fluorocarbon (12-17 lb)
  • Jigs and worms (stained water) → Braid with fluoro leader (30 lb + 12-15 lb leader)
  • Spinnerbaits → Monofilament (12-17 lb) or braid with leader
  • Ice fishing → Fluorocarbon or braid with fluoro leader
  • Beginner all-around → Monofilament (8-12 lb)

Quick Line Strength Guide

  • 2-6 lb: Panfish, trout, crappie
  • 6-10 lb: Smallmouth bass, walleye, larger trout
  • 10-15 lb: General bass fishing, pike
  • 15-25 lb: Big bass, muskies, salmon, heavy cover
  • 30-65 lb: Flipping heavy cover, trophy pike/muskie, deep trolling
