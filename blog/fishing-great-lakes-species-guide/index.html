Fishing the Great Lakes: A Complete Species-by-Species Guide

A complete guide to fishing the Great Lakes — Lake Ontario, Erie, Huron, Michigan, and Superior. Species, locations, and techniques for each lake.

The Great Lakes — Superior, Michigan, Huron, Erie, and Ontario — hold 20% of the world's freshwater and support some of the most diverse and productive fisheries in North America. Whether you're fishing from shore, a boat, or through the ice, each lake offers something unique.

Here's a species-by-species breakdown of what to fish for in each Great Lake, when to go, and how to catch them.

Lake Ontario

Key Species: Chinook Salmon, Coho Salmon, Lake Trout, Smallmouth Bass, Walleye

Lake Ontario offers world-class salmon and trout fishing, particularly along the southern Ontario shoreline. The lake is stocked heavily with Chinook and Coho salmon, which grow quickly on the abundant alewife population. Spring and summer trolling with downriggers and spoons is the standard technique. The Niagara River Bar, the Oakville to Bronte area, and the Bay of Quinte are top locations. Smallmouth bass fishing is excellent around the Thousand Islands region in the eastern basin.

Lake Erie

Key Species: Walleye, Yellow Perch, Smallmouth Bass, Steelhead

Lake Erie is the shallowest and warmest of the Great Lakes, making it the most biologically productive. The western basin is famous for its incredible walleye fishery — the best in the world. The eastern basin offers excellent smallmouth bass fishing around the rocky islands and shoals. Yellow perch are abundant throughout the lake and support a major commercial and recreational fishery. Steelhead (rainbow trout) run into tributary rivers like the Grand River in spring and fall.

Lake Huron

Key Species: Lake Trout, Chinook Salmon, Walleye, Smallmouth Bass, Yellow Perch

Lake Huron, including Georgian Bay and the North Channel, offers diverse fishing opportunities. The main basin is known for lake trout and Chinook salmon, particularly around the Bruce Peninsula. Georgian Bay is famous for its smallmouth bass fishing — the rocky, island-dotted waters provide ideal habitat. The St. Marys River and the area around Sault Ste. Marie offer excellent walleye fishing. Lake Huron's clear water requires lighter leaders and more natural presentations.

Lake Michigan

Key Species: Chinook Salmon, Coho Salmon, Lake Trout, Steelhead, Brown Trout

Lake Michigan (entirely in the US) offers exceptional salmon and trout fishing. The lake is stocked with millions of salmonids each year, supporting a world-class charter fishing industry. Spring and early summer are prime time for Chinook and Coho, particularly along the Wisconsin and Michigan shorelines. The lake also supports excellent steelhead and brown trout fishing in the tributary rivers during fall and spring spawning runs.

Lake Superior

Key Species: Lake Trout, Steelhead, Coho Salmon, Brook Trout, Whitefish

Lake Superior is the largest, deepest, and coldest of the Great Lakes. It's known for its lake trout fishery — the lake holds native lake trout populations that can exceed 40 pounds. The Canadian shoreline from Thunder Bay to Sault Ste. Marie offers excellent trolling opportunities. Steelhead and brook trout run into tributary rivers like the Kaministiquia and the Agawa. The lake's cold, clear water means fish are often deep — downriggers and heavy weights are essential for open-water fishing.

Recommended Gear for the Great Lakes

  • Salmon & Trout trolling — Downriggers, dipsey divers, and lead core line with spoons, flashers, and flies. A fish finder is essential.
  • Walleye — Crankbaits, worm harnesses, and jigs. Bottom bouncers for river fishing.
  • Smallmouth Bass — Tube jigs, drop-shot rigs, spinnerbaits, and topwater lures. Light to medium spinning gear.
  • Yellow Perch — Small jigs tipped with minnows or worms. Ultra-light spinning tackle.
  • Check regulations — Each Great Lake has specific seasons, size limits, and catch limits. Ontario's regulations differ from US state regulations.
