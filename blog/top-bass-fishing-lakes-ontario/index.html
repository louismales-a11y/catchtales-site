Top 10 Bass Fishing Lakes in Ontario

Ontario is home to some of the best bass fishing in North America. Here are the top lakes every angler should put on their list.

Ontario holds more than 250,000 lakes — more freshwater than any other province or state in North America. For bass anglers, that means endless opportunities to find trophy smallmouth and largemouth. Whether you're a weekend warrior or a tournament competitor, these ten lakes deliver consistent action and unforgettable days on the water.

1. Lake St. Clair

Species: Smallmouth Bass, Largemouth Bass

Best time: June through September

Lake St. Clair is widely considered one of the best smallmouth fisheries on the planet. The lake's shallow, weedy flats and rocky shoals hold massive schools of smallmouth that hit hard and fight harder. The St. Clair River delta and Anchor Bay are top spots. Largemouth are plentiful in the canals and marinas along the Ontario shoreline. With an average depth of only 10 feet, the lake warms quickly in spring and stays productive all summer. Many tournament circuits make regular stops here for good reason.

2. Lake Erie (Eastern Basin)

Species: Smallmouth Bass

Best time: May through October

The eastern basin of Lake Erie, particularly around Long Point and the St. Lawrence Seaway, offers world-class smallmouth bass fishing. Clear water, rocky shoals, and deep drop-offs create ideal habitat. The smallmouth here are incredibly aggressive and often exceed 5 pounds. Drift fishing with tube jigs or drop-shot rigs is the go-to technique. The western basin near Leamington is better for largemouth, especially in the marshes and shallow bays.

3. Lake Ontario (Bay of Quinte)

Species: Largemouth Bass, Smallmouth Bass

Best time: June to early October

The Bay of Quinte is famous for its walleye, but bass anglers know it's equally productive for both largemouth and smallmouth. The upper bay near Belleville and Trenton has extensive weedy shallows that hold good numbers of largemouth. The lower bay near Picton and the Adolphus Reach offers smallmouth fishing around rocky points and islands. The bay's stained water means fish relate closely to cover — target weed edges, docks, and rock piles.

4. Lake Muskoka

Species: Smallmouth Bass, Largemouth Bass

Best time: June through September

Part of Ontario's iconic cottage country, Lake Muskoka delivers classic Canadian Shield bass fishing. Clear, deep water with rocky shorelines, islands, and submerged boulders creates perfect smallmouth territory. Largemouth can be found in the weedy bays and around docks. The lake gets busy in July and August, but early morning and evening fishing is exceptional. Topwater action here is hard to beat.

5. Lake Nipissing

Species: Smallmouth Bass, Largemouth Bass

Best time: June to September

Lake Nipissing is a massive, shallow lake near North Bay that offers outstanding bass fishing with relatively light fishing pressure compared to southern Ontario lakes. The lake has hundreds of islands, rocky shoals, and weedy bays. Smallmouth are abundant around rocky structure, while largemouth prefer the weedy shallows and boggy areas along the north shore. The French River outlet is a particularly productive area.

6. Rainy Lake

Species: Smallmouth Bass

Best time: June through August

Located on the Ontario-Minnesota border, Rainy Lake offers remote, pristine smallmouth fishing. The water is clear and cold, and the smallmouth are plentiful and strong. The countless islands, rocky points, and deep channels provide perfect habitat. This is a great lake for a multi-day fishing trip — many fly-in and boat-in outposts operate here. The scenery alone makes it worth the trip.

7. Lake of the Woods

Species: Smallmouth Bass, Largemouth Bass

Best time: June to early September

Lake of the Woods straddles the Ontario-Manitoba border and offers over 14,000 islands and 65,000 miles of shoreline. Smallmouth fishing is excellent throughout, particularly around rocky structures and islands. Largemouth are more localized but can be found in the warmer, weedy bays. The lake is vast — having a good map or GPS is essential. Many anglers spend a full week here and still don't cover it all.

8. Rice Lake

Species: Largemouth Bass, Smallmouth Bass

Best time: May through October

Rice Lake, located just north of Cobourg, is one of southern Ontario's most accessible and productive bass lakes. It's relatively shallow with abundant weed growth, making it prime largemouth territory. The lake is dotted with islands, weed beds, and submerged structure. Smallmouth are present around the few rocky areas and deeper channels. Rice Lake gets fishing pressure but consistently produces quality bass. It's a popular tournament venue for good reason.

9. Lake Simcoe

Species: Smallmouth Bass, Largemouth Bass

Best time: June to October

Lake Simcoe is one of Ontario's most popular year-round fisheries. Smallmouth dominate the main lake, relating to rocky shoals, weed lines, and drop-offs in 10-25 feet of water. Kempenfelt Bay and Cook's Bay are particularly productive. Largemouth are concentrated in the weedy shallows of the south end around Georgina Island and the Holland River marshes. The lake gets busy, but there's plenty of water to explore.

10. St. Lawrence River (Thousand Islands Region)

Species: Smallmouth Bass, Largemouth Bass

Best time: June through September

The Thousand Islands stretch of the St. Lawrence River offers incredible smallmouth fishing amid stunning scenery. The clear, clean water and rocky structure create ideal habitat. Smallmouth are abundant around the islands, shoals, and channel edges. Largemouth prefer the quieter back bays and weedy areas. The current can be strong in the main channels, so heavier presentations are often needed. This is a world-class smallmouth destination that rivals any lake in North America.

Tips for Bass Fishing in Ontario

  • Get a licence — All anglers 18+ need an Ontario fishing licence. Purchase online at ontario.ca/fishing.
  • Check regulations — Bass seasons, size limits, and catch limits vary by zone. Some lakes have catch-and-release only for bass.
  • Use the right gear — A medium-heavy rod with 10-17 lb test line covers most situations. Bring spinning and baitcasting setups.
  • Match the hatch — Tube jigs, drop-shot rigs, spinnerbaits, and topwater lures are Ontario bass staples. Crayfish-pattern tubes are hard to beat for smallmouth.
  • Watch the weather — Ontario weather changes fast. A stable barometer and overcast skies often produce the best bites.
